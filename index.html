About Me

Committed to bridging the gap between research and patient care

I hold a Bachelor of Science in Health Sciences from the University of Waterloo (2024), where I built a strong foundation in evidence-based practice, population health, and health communication. I am currently completing my MHSc Capstone at The Hospital for Sick Children (SickKids) in Toronto.

Project Description

Improving Awareness in the Absence of Ovarian Cancer Screening

This capstone project was designed to address a significant and underrecognized public health communication problem: many women and even some providers believe that Pap smears screen for ovarian cancer - they do not. This misconception, and a broader lack of awareness about ovarian cancer symptoms and the absence of a population screening program, contributes to delays in diagnosis and poorer outcomes.

The project proceeded in two major phases: first, a rigorous scoping review of existing Canadian and international literature and grey literature; and second, the development of a multi-component knowledge mobilization toolkit designed to bridge the identified gaps for both patients and providers.

Methodological Framework

Arksey and O'Malley (2005) scoping review framework with PRISMA-ScR (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses - Scoping Reviews extension) reporting guidance. Databases searched: PubMed, CINAHL, Web of Science.

Learning Outcome 1 - Scoping Review

Knowledge Mobilization Gaps in
Ovarian Cancer Detection Communication

This scoping review systematically mapped the existing evidence on how ovarian cancer detection information is communicated in the absence of a population screening program. Using the Arksey and O'Malley (2005) framework and PRISMA-ScR guidance, the review searched three major databases - PubMed, CINAHL, and Web of Science - to identify studies addressing awareness, messaging, and knowledge mobilization gaps.

Key findings included widespread inconsistencies in symptom messaging across organizations (e.g., varying thresholds for when to seek care), a common misconception that Pap smears detect ovarian cancer, and structural gaps in how research evidence is translated into accessible patient and provider communication.

The review was reviewed by Dr. Barwick and identified as strong enough for potential publication, with minor final revisions completed in April 2026.

Initial Reflection - Entering the Capstone

Beginning this capstone, I carried both motivation and uncertainty. My interest in women's health and ovarian cancer awareness was personal as much as academic - I was aware of how little public knowledge exists about the absence of a screening program. Entering Dr. Barwick's lab introduced me to a rigorous research environment that challenged me to move beyond surface-level understanding and engage with implementation science as a discipline. Early on, I recognized gaps in my own knowledge of KM methodology and committed to developing these skills through the process.

Mid-Point Reflection - Navigating Complexity

The shift from knowledge translation to knowledge mobilization - guided by Dr. Barwick's feedback - was a pivotal moment. It required me to revisit foundational assumptions about the project and rebuild with greater clarity. This experience reinforced my understanding that strong research requires intellectual flexibility and the willingness to reframe. My meeting with Ovarian Cancer Canada program leads was equally formative: it grounded the academic work in real patient experiences, including the challenge of long wait times and inconsistent symptom messaging across providers.

Final Reflection - Critical Self-Reflection

My capstone experience was one of the most meaningful parts of my graduate program because it allowed me to take a topic that was personally and professionally important to me and turn it into an applied knowledge mobilization project. My most significant learning was that knowledge mobilization is not just about sharing information; it is about making information usable, clear, and connected to real decisions. The full reflection traces my growth across all five Applied Health Sciences competencies, the strengths I drew on, the limitations I encountered, and where I want to grow next.
